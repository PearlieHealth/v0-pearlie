## Is this a dental emergency?

Some problems need urgent care within hours. Others can wait a day or two.

**Seek urgent dental care immediately if you have:**

- Severe toothache not controlled by painkillers
- A knocked-out or partially dislodged tooth
- Facial swelling, especially near the eye or neck
- A painful dental abscess
- Uncontrolled bleeding from the mouth
- Trauma to the jaw

**Go to A&E immediately if:**

- Swelling affects your breathing or swallowing
- Bleeding will not stop with pressure
- You suspect a jaw fracture

If you're unsure, it's safer to get assessed.

## What to do while you arrange to see a dentist

The following advice is based on [NHS guidance](https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/toothache/).

### Toothache

You can:

- Take painkillers such as paracetamol or ibuprofen (if suitable for you)
- Rinse your mouth with warm salt water
- Apply a cold compress to the outside of your cheek

See [NHS toothache guidance](https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/toothache/) for more detail.

### Dental abscess

You should:

- See a dentist urgently
- Take painkillers if needed
- Avoid very hot or cold food and drinks

Do not attempt to burst the abscess yourself. If swelling spreads to your eye, neck, or affects breathing or swallowing, go to A&E immediately.

See [NHS dental abscess guidance](https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/dental-abscess/) for more detail.

### Knocked-out tooth (adult tooth only)

If a permanent tooth has been knocked out:

- Pick it up by the crown (white part), not the root
- Rinse it briefly if dirty — do not scrub
- Try to gently push it back into the socket if possible
- If you can't replant it, store it in milk and seek urgent dental care

Time is critical — see a dentist within 30 minutes for the best chance of saving the tooth.

See [NHS knocked-out tooth guidance](https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/knocked-out-tooth/) for more detail.

### Broken or chipped tooth

- Rinse your mouth with warm water
- Apply gauze to any bleeding with gentle pressure
- Use a cold compress on the outside of your cheek
- Save any broken pieces and bring them to your appointment

**Important:** This information is general guidance only and does not replace professional assessment.

## What to expect at an emergency dental appointment

1. **Assessment** — The dentist examines the affected area and takes x-rays if needed
2. **Pain management** — Local anaesthetic is administered to numb the area
3. **Immediate treatment** — This might be draining an abscess, placing a temporary filling, stabilising a broken tooth, or performing an extraction
4. **Prescription** — Antibiotics and/or stronger painkillers if required
5. **Follow-up plan** — The dentist explains what further treatment is needed and schedules follow-up appointments

Most emergency dental visits take 30–60 minutes. The goal is to stabilise the situation and manage pain, with definitive treatment planned for a subsequent appointment.

If a tooth cannot be saved, [dental implants](/treatments/dental-implants) are the gold standard for permanent replacement.

## Preventing dental emergencies

While not all emergencies are preventable, you can reduce your risk:

- **Regular dental check-ups** — catch problems before they become emergencies
- **Good oral hygiene** — brush twice daily, floss daily, use fluoride toothpaste
- **Wear a mouthguard** — for contact sports or if you grind your teeth at night
- **Don't use teeth as tools** — avoid opening bottles, tearing packaging, or biting nails
- **Address problems early** — don't ignore sensitivity, mild toothache, or a loose filling

The most effective prevention is keeping up with regular dental appointments. Many emergencies could have been avoided with earlier intervention.

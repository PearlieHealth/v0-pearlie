## When is it a dental emergency?

Not all dental problems need immediate attention, but some require urgent care within hours. Here's how to tell the difference:

**See an emergency dentist immediately:**
- Severe toothache not controlled by painkillers
- A tooth that has been knocked out or partially dislodged
- A broken, cracked, or chipped tooth (especially with pain)
- Facial swelling, particularly if spreading towards the eye or neck
- Uncontrolled bleeding from the mouth
- A dental abscess (painful swelling with pus)
- Injury to the jaw

**Can wait 24–48 hours (see a dentist soon, not emergency):**
- A lost filling or crown (without pain)
- Mild toothache that responds to painkillers
- A small chip without sharp edges or pain
- Sensitivity to hot or cold
- Minor gum bleeding

**Go to A&E if:**
- Facial swelling is affecting your breathing or swallowing
- You have uncontrolled bleeding that won't stop with pressure
- You've experienced facial trauma or a suspected jaw fracture

## What to do right now

If you're experiencing a dental emergency, here's what to do while you arrange to see a dentist:

**For severe toothache:**
- Take ibuprofen (if you can) — it reduces inflammation as well as pain
- Apply a cold compress to the outside of your cheek
- Rinse gently with warm salt water
- Avoid very hot, cold, or sweet foods and drinks
- Do not place aspirin directly on the gum — this can cause a chemical burn

**For a knocked-out tooth:**
- Find the tooth and handle it by the crown only (the white part)
- If it's dirty, rinse briefly with milk — do not scrub or remove tissue
- Try to gently push it back into the socket and bite on a clean cloth
- If you can't replant it, store it in milk, saliva, or a tooth preservation kit
- See a dentist within 30 minutes for the best chance of saving the tooth

**For a broken or chipped tooth:**
- Rinse your mouth with warm water
- Apply gauze to any bleeding areas with gentle pressure
- Use a cold compress on the outside of your cheek to reduce swelling
- Save any broken pieces and bring them to your appointment
- Cover sharp edges with dental wax or sugar-free chewing gum

**For an abscess or facial swelling:**
- Rinse with warm salt water several times a day
- Take painkillers as directed
- Do not try to drain or burst the abscess yourself
- If swelling spreads rapidly or affects breathing — go to A&E immediately

## Types of dental emergencies

### Toothache

The most common dental emergency. Causes range from deep cavities and cracked teeth to gum infections and abscesses. Emergency treatment focuses on diagnosing the cause (with an x-ray) and managing pain. Follow-up treatment may involve fillings, root canal, or extraction.

### Broken or chipped tooth

A broken tooth needs prompt assessment to determine if the nerve is exposed. Minor chips may only need smoothing or bonding. Larger breaks may require a crown, veneer, or root canal treatment. Keep any broken pieces and see a dentist as soon as possible.

### Knocked-out tooth

A permanently knocked-out (avulsed) adult tooth can often be saved if replanted within 30–60 minutes. Baby teeth should not be replanted. The dentist will splint the tooth in place and monitor healing over the following weeks.

### Dental abscess

An abscess is a bacterial infection that causes a pocket of pus to form around a tooth root or in the gum. Symptoms include throbbing pain, swelling, fever, and a bad taste. Treatment involves draining the abscess, antibiotics, and addressing the underlying cause (usually root canal or extraction).

### Lost filling or crown

A lost filling or crown exposes the underlying tooth to bacteria and sensitivity. While not always an emergency, it should be addressed within 24–48 hours. As a temporary measure, dental cement (available from pharmacies) can cover the area until you see your dentist.

## How to find an emergency dentist

**Option 1: NHS 111**
Call 111 (free, available 24/7). They'll assess your symptoms and direct you to the nearest available out-of-hours dental service. This is the best route for NHS emergency care.

**Option 2: Your regular dentist**
If you're registered with a dental practice, call them first — even outside normal hours, many practices have an emergency answerphone message with instructions or an out-of-hours number.

**Option 3: Private emergency clinics**
Private dental clinics in most cities offer same-day emergency appointments. Costs are higher than NHS, but appointments are generally easier to secure at short notice.

## What to expect at an emergency dental appointment

1. **Assessment** — The dentist examines the affected area and takes x-rays if needed
2. **Pain management** — Local anaesthetic is administered to numb the area
3. **Immediate treatment** — This might be draining an abscess, placing a temporary filling, stabilising a broken tooth, or performing an extraction
4. **Prescription** — Antibiotics and/or stronger painkillers if required
5. **Follow-up plan** — The dentist explains what further treatment is needed and schedules follow-up appointments

Most emergency dental visits take 30–60 minutes. The goal is to stabilise the situation and manage pain, with definitive treatment planned for a subsequent appointment.

## Preventing dental emergencies

While not all emergencies are preventable, you can reduce your risk:

- **Regular dental check-ups** — catch problems before they become emergencies
- **Good oral hygiene** — brush twice daily, floss daily, use fluoride toothpaste
- **Wear a mouthguard** — for contact sports or if you grind your teeth at night
- **Don't use teeth as tools** — avoid opening bottles, tearing packaging, or biting nails
- **Address problems early** — don't ignore sensitivity, mild toothache, or a loose filling
- **Limit sugary food and drinks** — sugar feeds the bacteria that cause decay and abscesses

The most effective prevention is keeping up with regular dental appointments. Many emergencies could have been avoided with earlier intervention.
